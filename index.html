Trained on the most recent 5 years of data — updated monthly so the model always reflects current market behavior, not outdated patterns.
Data Sources Verified 2017–2026 LTR: DAT Freight & Analytics, sourced via Ryan Transportation monthly dry van reports. Data starts January 2017 — earlier history was excluded because it could not be verified.

WHLSL: U.S. wholesale durable goods sales, published monthly by the Federal Reserve (FRED: WHLSLRMPCIMSA). Used with a 2-month lag.
Test 1 — Walk-Forward Backtest Primary Why: We needed to know if the model actually works in the real world — not just on the same data it was trained on. The only honest way to test a forecast model is to pretend you're in the past, make a prediction without looking at the future, then check if you were right.

How: We ran 29 forecasts across January 2023 – May 2025. For each one, the model was only allowed to use data that would have existed at that moment in time. No cheating.

What it showed:
79.3%of the time it correctly called up or down
29independent real-world tests

What it means: Nearly 8 out of 10 times, the model correctly identified whether freight demand would tighten or soften 10 months later. That is a meaningful edge for pricing decisions — even though the exact number won't always be right, knowing the direction usually is.
Test 2 — Clark-West Test Significance Why: Adding wholesale sales data to the model makes it more complex. We needed to prove that complexity is worth it — that WHLSL actually improves the forecast, and the improvement isn't just luck.

How: We built two versions of the model — one using only current LTR, one adding WHLSL. We ran both on the same 29 historical forecasts and compared how far off each one was. We then asked: if wholesale sales had no real effect, how likely would we be to see results this good just by chance?

What it showed: Less than 1% chance the improvement was luck.
p = 0.007standard test
p = 0.004conservative version

What it means: A p-value below 0.05 means there's less than a 5% chance the improvement happened by random chance. Both versions cleared that bar — including the stricter version that accounts for overlapping forecast errors. WHLSL genuinely improves the forecast.
Test 3 — Coefficient Stability Consistency Why: A model that works in one time period but falls apart in another isn't reliable. We needed to check whether the WHLSL relationship held consistently — or whether it was a fluke of a particular data window.

How: We retrained the model 29 times using different time periods and checked whether wholesale sales always pushed the forecast in the same direction — or whether it sometimes said one thing and sometimes said the opposite.

What it showed:
29/29same direction every time
27/29statistically significant

What it means: In every single refit — across different markets, different years, different economic conditions — WHLSL pointed the same way. That consistency is strong evidence the relationship is real, not a coincidence.
Test 4 — Residual Diagnostics Integrity Why: Even a model that gets the direction right can have hidden problems — like consistently overestimating, underestimating, or being unreliable in specific market conditions. This test checks for those issues.

How: We looked at the model's errors across all 29 forecasts — checking for systematic bias, patterns in the mistakes, and whether accuracy varied by market regime.

What it showed:
· No significant bias — the model doesn't systematically over- or under-predict (p = 0.111)
· Errors are correlated — but expected: a 10-month forecast naturally overlaps with the next month's forecast, so errors move together. Already corrected for in Test 2.
· Accuracy by market type: Low LTR → 90% · Middle LTR → 89% · High LTR → 60%

What it means: The model is well-behaved in normal conditions. Its main weakness is elevated markets like today — which is why the dashboard clearly flags lower confidence in High-LTR regimes.
Forecast Range Stress Range Why: A single point forecast (6.59) gives a false sense of precision. We needed a way to communicate the realistic spread of outcomes — and make it wider when the model has historically struggled, narrower when it hasn't.

How: We measured how wrong the model has been historically across all 29 forecasts, then scaled the range wider or narrower depending on the current market type. In High-LTR conditions the model's errors have been 1.29× larger than average, so the range widens accordingly.

What it means: The blue band on the chart is not a guaranteed probability range — it's a planning zone showing where LTR has historically landed in comparable situations. In today's High-LTR environment it covered all 10 comparable historical outcomes, but the sample is small and the same data was used to build it, so treat it as a guide, not a guarantee.
Known Limitations Caveats We're slightly outside validated territory. Today's LTR of 9.10 is above the highest level the model was validated on (8.84). The model is extrapolating — directional signal may still hold, but the point estimate is less reliable.

Small sample in today's market type. Only 10 of 29 test forecasts came from High-LTR conditions. The 60% accuracy figure is real but uncertain — with 10 observations, even a small change in outcomes would move that number significantly.

Surprises aren't modeled. Tariff changes, severe weather, geopolitical events — these aren't in the model. The 2025 freight spike was a good example: the model missed it because no macro indicator predicted it in advance.

6.59 is a direction, not a target. The model's proven value is telling you which way the market is heading — not the exact number it will land on.
